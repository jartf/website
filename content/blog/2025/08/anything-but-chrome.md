Okay, let's get this straight right off the bat: I do not use Chrome. I don't touch it. I don't keep it around as a backup "just in case". Heck, I don't even let it sit on my machines like some ugly pest hogging space in the corner. If you see a laptop and the first icon on the dock is that clown-colored wheel, odds are the person behind it didn't really choose Chrome. Chrome chose them. And that's the whole problem. It's not a browser anymore, it's a default state of existence, like gravity or taxes. People don't think about why they use it. They just... do.

And performance? Chrome isn't even the fastest browser anymore, though people still cling to that idea like it's 2010. Even Microsoft Edge, the butt of internet jokes for years, is faster than Chrome these days. Imagine being slower than Edge. Yikes. Even Microsoft can make a browser that people might actually tolerate, which is hilarious and sad at the same time. Chrome guzzles RAM like it's free beer night and lags out the second you open more than a handful of tabs. The only thing it's still number one at is burning your resources while pretending to be cutting-edge, oh and some market share too while we're at it.

But speed isn’t even the main issue. People will shrug and say, "Oh but it's fine because Chrome integrates with Google services." Great, that's exactly the problem. That integration isn't convenience, it's dependency. It's not just a browser anymore, it's now the spearhead of a business model that wants to be the internet. Search, ads, email, maps, videos, cloud storage, documents, browsers. Google's ideal future is one where you never leave their ecosystem. You search on Google, you watch on YouTube, you email in Gmail, you navigate with Maps, and the entire time you're sealed in their Chrome bubble, where every tab and every scroll gets sucked into their surveillance machine. And people keep telling me, "But it's convenient." Yeah, convenient if you're okay with voluntarily being colonized by a corporation that thinks privacy is a suggestion.

And don't get me started on FLoC. Or, okay, the new "Topics" API or Privacy Sandbox or whatever the hell they're calling it now after everyone called them out. I wrote a [whole blog post](https://web.archive.org/web/20221004065548/https://raeexpe.xyz/2021/08/googles-floc-what-it-is-why-its-bad-and-how-to-opt-out-of-it/) on my old website when Google first unveiled FLoC, because it was one of the most blatant power grabs I've ever seen. Them replacing third-party cookies with FLoC was less about privacy and more about them saying, "Hey, let's surveil you in a way that's slightly less obvious but way more invasive." Third-party cookies are invasive, sure, but at least they're fragmented. There are different ad companies and different trackers competing for your crumbs, and they at least allowed some amount of separation between your data and advertisers. FLoC, Topics, whatever next-gen scheme Google rolls out—they're just ways for Google to squeeze every bit of behavioral data out of you while pretending it's okay because "privacy-preserving". Newsflash: it isn't. It's still Google. And it still wants to own everything.

So yeah, Chrome gets zero points for privacy, but it also gets zero points for accountability. Google can mess with your browser, tweak APIs, and roll out half-baked surveillance tech without any consequences because the average user doesn't even know the difference. You can bet that any small loophole, quirk, or opt-out setting will be buried under fifty different menus and confusing explanations. Chrome isn't about you, it's about Google. That's why I left. That's why I refuse to go back.

Which brings me to my actual browsers of choice: Librewolf and Brave. Now, do I think Librewolf or Brave are perfect saviors? No. But they at least have the decency to not spit in my face while telling me it's raining. Librewolf is the kind of browser that makes you feel like you're actually in control of your online life. It's Firefox under the hood, but stripped of telemetry, preconfigured for privacy, and built by people who actually care about the user, not the ad revenue. It demands some effort: you might need to flip switches, fix breakages, actually know what you're doing. But I'd rather deal with that, because now I can actually breathe while browsing. It's beautiful in its own way.

Brave is the other tool I use, and yes, it's... complicated. Yes, yes, I know the CEO has a history of being openly transphobic and plenty of people think that makes the whole browser unacceptable. And yeah, I'm not excusing that, it's gross. But I'm also not the kind of person who boycotts an entire piece of software because of one person at the top. If I did that, I wouldn't be using half the products on the market, because a lot of corporations are run by people with blood on their hands. Brave, as a product, does its job: it blocks ads and trackers natively, it gives me control, it's blazing fast, and it doesn't treat my browsing as raw material to be auctioned off. I don't use the crypto garbage, I don't care about the BAT tokens or whatever "Web3" stuff they do next, I just use it because it's practical. I can take the good, apply it to my life, and ignore the toxicity at the top.

Meanwhile, Chrome is like this overambitious, corporate nightmare that wants to replace every browser, every cookie, every tracking method, every little third-party innovation you thought was private. And yes, I get it, some people live in Google's ecosystem and can't imagine life without Gmail syncing, Google Docs, or Drive integration. Cool. Not my problem. I'd rather make my life slightly more inconvenient than give them another inch of control. And for what? Speed? Lol, even Edge is faster, Firefox derivatives like Librewolf are fine, Brave is fine, Vivaldi if you want some niche luxury, pick your poison really. Anything is better than Chrome at this point.

So when people ask why I'm so adamant about "anything but Chrome", this is why. It's not just about what's running on my screen, it's about who owns my screen. It's me saying I refuse to contribute to the monopolization of the web by a single company. It's me saying I value privacy, speed, and user agency over convenience and corporate appeasement.

So yeah, I'll happily daily drive Librewolf, I'll use Brave when I want Chromium speed without selling my soul, and hell, sometimes even Edge when I'm forced onto my university's Office 365 stuff. Anything but Chrome. Because Chrome doesn't just want to be a browser, it wants to be the internet. And I don't trust any one company, let alone Google, with that much power. No browser is perfect, no tool is, but it's better than feeding the Google beast.
