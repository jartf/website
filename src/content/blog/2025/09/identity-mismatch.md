Sometimes I feel like my whole identity is one long mismatch. The kind of mismatch that doesn't blow up in your face, but quietly eats at you, like wearing shoes that are half a size too small. You can walk in them, you can even run in them if you need to, but by the end of the day your feet still ache, and you wonder why you settled for the wrong pair in the first place. That's how I feel about my major, and, honestly, about the way I'm seen in general.

I study international economics at a diplomatic academy. It looks neat on paper, very serious, very purposeful, prestigious even. People hear it and go, "Ah, so you're going to be a diplomat, or a trade negotiator, or some UN official." It carries a certain gravitas, let's say, and it's the kind of career label that makes aunts and uncles nod with approval. But the irony is, I've never really felt like that person. I don't wake up with visions of myself shaking hands in suits, maneuvering through conferences, or networking my way up some global ladder. I wake up thinking about systems, connections, patterns.

It's funny how much weight other people's perceptions carry. When people look at me, they don't see "future economist" or "foreign policy guy" immediately. Instead, what they see is... IT. Communications. Back office. Nerdy guy. The person behind the screen that makes sure your Wifi works, or the one writing copy for some kind of campaign. But when people say it, weirdly, I like that. And it doesn't feel wrong. If anything, it feels more right than economist or diplomat. It's like they're seeing something I wasn't brave enough to admit to myself. That deep down, I'd rather be the one tinkering with structures in the background than standing in front of a crowd performing diplomacy. I can do the latter if I really have to, sure, but capable and comfortable are not the same thing.

What complicates it is that the mismatch isn't a disaster. I don't hate economics. I don't sit in class thinking, "I want to quit", although I sometimes do wish I studied something else instead. It's more like being at a party where you know everyone, you can chat and laugh and even enjoy yourself, but you're aware that it's not your party. The vibe is off. It's not home. You can get through the night and even look good doing it, but your mind constantly goes "Should I even be here?"

And it's not just the field. It's identity in general. The way people see me versus the way I see myself versus the way I want to be. I'm fluent in my field, but I don't look the part. It kinda feels like I'm forever miscast in my own story. And it's exhausting sometimes, because you really start second-guessing yourself. For me, it did get to a point where I would wonder if any of us really match our external image. Maybe we're all walking contradictions, studying one thing, working in another, looking like one but secretly dreaming of being another. Maybe mismatch is the default, and the people who seem perfectly aligned are just really good actors. Or, am I in the wrong field altogether? Hell, is everyone else just lazy with their categories and projecting their stereotypes onto me because it's easier than trying to read someone with contradictions?

The identity dissonance feels heavier because of how I process people. I'm introverted, I'm the type who'd rather sit behind the scenes and organizing, observing, and fixing stuff instead. I'm not exactly good at real life conversations, and I can't "own the room" like some of my friends do. I've never been the type to chase connections just for the sake of networking. I'd rather cut off anyone who doesn't align with me than pretend. That makes me efficient with relationships, but it also makes me hyper-aware of the "performance" culture that fields like diplomacy demand. You're expected to keep everyone on the hook, smile through the dissonance, shake the hands even of people you'd rather never meet again. And I can do that, but it feels like selling a piece of myself every time. So when people tell me I "look like an IT guy", or that I give off the vibe of someone who belongs in back-office ops, I don't take it as an insult. If anything, I cling to it like proof that I'm not crazy. Proof that my internal self leaks out despite the mask of my major. It's oddly comforting to be seen that way, even if it doesn't match the official story of my life.

But the thing is, I'm not fully one thing or the other.
If I were really a future diplomat, maybe I'd feel hyped and energized by the performance of it all. After all the handshakes and debates and international summits are alluring.
If I were purely a tech person, maybe I'd feel comfortable embracing that identity and owning the nerdy vibe. I'd dive even deeper into systems and code.
But instead, I have to live in this in-between zone, where what I'm doing with my degree doesn't exactly reflect the persona people often assign me, nor my skillset and abilities, and not even my inner reality either. And that gap creates a constant static every time I think about it.

But here's where it gets weird. There's a part of me that likes the mismatch. Maybe because it makes me feel like I'm not easy to categorize into a "basket". People hear "economics" and expect one thing, but then they meet me and it doesn't add up. I become a glitch in the system, and I kind of enjoy that. It means I can slide between different spaces and surprise people. It's liberating to not be the stereotype of your field. Like, maybe I'm not in the wrong room after all, maybe I'm just the person who makes the room feel a little off-balanced.

Still, the thought lingers. Do you ever sit with the idea that your life could have gone another way if you had just chosen differently at one path in the road? If I had leaned harder into tech or communications, or I dunno, maybe even writing, would I feel more "at home" now? Or would I be restless anyway, just mismatched in a different costume? Because I suspect the mismatch isn't just about the major. It's about me. My tendency to live in the in-between. Not fully this, not fully that. Introverted but not exactly shy. Studying economics but looking like IT. Living independently but not fully escaping hell. Vietnamese but not fully enough. It's all a mismatch. And maybe that's just who I am, a person who never lands squarely in one box.

And maybe that's the heart of it. This might not just be about me being in a field that doesn't match how others see me, rather it's about the weirdness of living with layers that don't align. I'm simultaneously too nerdy for economics, too pragmatic for communications, too reserved for diplomacy, and too people-aware to disappear fully into the back-office roles people assign me. It's a tension I haven't resolved yet, and maybe I never will. But I keep thinking that maybe the mismatch isn't a flaw. Maybe it's the one thing that will keep me sane in a world where people either fit too neatly into their boxes or force themselves into roles they secretly hate. Maybe that identity mismatch isn't something to fix, it's something to live with. Like static on a radio, it never goes away, but you learn to listen through it. Maybe I'll never feel like I perfectly belong in my field, but maybe belonging was never the point. Maybe the point is to take that mismatch and make something new out of it. To be the economist who doesn't look like one, the diplomat who doesn't talk like one, the person who walks into the wrong room and decides to stay anyway.

Because, well, who knows what will happen really? Sometimes the wrong room ends up being where the best conversations happen. Or at least I think so.
